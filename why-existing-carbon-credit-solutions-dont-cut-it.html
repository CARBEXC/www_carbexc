The Problem With Current Carbon Credit Solutions

Luca 31.12.2021

Demand for carbon credits has been increasing year-over-year as more than 1600 companies have committed to Net Zero, of which 20% are part of the 2000 largest companies worldwide, with a $14T market cap.

If you add to that the fact that 1.6 GtCO2e residual emissions need to be removed from the atmosphere, you would think that there is a plan and that there are some great solutions out there for us to get there.

The reality could not be further from the truth, here is why.

The current solutions for carbon credits are focused on offsetting over removal.

When it comes to reaching net zero, one major issue is that carbon removal is sometimes mistaken or conflated with carbon offsetting. In actuality, carbon offsetting and carbon removal provide two very distinct outcomes. When one metric tonne of CO2 is emitted, another metric tonne of CO2 is prevented somewhere else, resulting in a net increase in CO2 emissions. When one metric tonne of CO2 is released, one metric tonne is totally removed from the atmosphere by carbon removal.

According to the Taskforce on Scaling Voluntary Carbon Markets, 2 gigatons of carbon reduction is required by 2030. To achieve this we will need a 15-fold increase from 2019 figures in volunteer effort by 2030. Making it a compelling case for increasing carbon removal.

The certification process lacks accuracy, transparency and accountability.

The fact that there is no formal international standard for carbon offset accounting is at the foundation of most of the difficulty. Instead, commercial organisations such as Gold Standard, Verra, and the American Carbon Registry calculate, certify, and broker offsets using a mix of methodologies in each part of the process.

A research paper published in 2021 examined 100 offsets certified by Gold Standard and other organisations. Offsets from projects supporting forest conservation, climate-friendly farming, direct air capture technology, and other methods for reducing emissions. They discovered that 90% of the offsets either didn't offset as much as they claimed, aren't permanent, had negative consequences for local populations or ecosystems, or were a mix of the above.
